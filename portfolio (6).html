Project 2 — Analysis of a Data-Driven System

How Effective Are Instagram's Parental Control Tools in Supporting the Online Well-Being and Safety of Teens Aged 13–14?

Data-Driven AnalysisRhetorical AnalysisCritical ResearchDigital Literacy

This project is an in-depth analysis of Instagram's parental control system — a data-driven platform designed to protect teens aged 13–14 through features such as sensitive content filters, screen-time monitoring, and automated safety notices. The objective was to evaluate whether these tools genuinely fulfill their stated purpose of keeping young users safe online. To answer this question, I combined rhetorical analysis of Instagram's official communications — including statements from Head of Instagram Adam Mosseri — with a critical review of user feedback, independent research from Northeastern University, and reporting by journalists covering child safety advocacy. My analysis revealed a significant gap between the platform's promises and real-world performance, with evidence suggesting that over 80% of Instagram's safety features for teens do not function as advertised. This project demonstrates my ability to examine a complex digital system from both a technical and humanistic perspective, apply critical thinking to evaluate institutional claims, and communicate evidence-based findings clearly — skills directly relevant to careers in policy, education, publishing, and public advocacy.

Project 3 — Seminar Paper

The Limits of User-Dependent Safety: Instagram's Parental Controls for Young Teens

Academic WritingComparative ResearchScholarly AnalysisArgumentation

This seminar paper extends the analysis begun in Project 2 into a full scholarly argument, contending that Instagram's parental control tools are structurally insufficient — not because parents fail to use them, but because the platform's engagement-driven architecture fundamentally prioritizes attention capture over teen well-being. Drawing on a human-centered analytical framework, the paper engages with scholars including Safiya Noble, Shoshana Zuboff, Laurence Steinberg, and Sonia Livingstone to ground its critique in both critical technology studies and developmental psychology. A comparative analysis of TikTok's Family Pairing, YouTube's Supervised Accounts, and third-party monitoring app Bark reveals that Instagram's approach remains reactive and opt-in where competitors have moved toward enforceable, automated protections. The paper proposes a multi-layered reform framework distributing safety responsibility across platforms, policymakers, educators, and families. This project demonstrates my ability to synthesize interdisciplinary scholarship, construct and defend a sustained argument, evaluate counterarguments fairly, and communicate complex policy issues with precision.

Project 4 — Senior Symposium Poster & Presentation

Senior Symposium Poster: The Limits of User-Dependent Safety — Instagram's Parental Controls for Young Teens

Public CommunicationVisual DesignResearch SynthesisPolicy Proposal

This Senior Symposium poster presents the culminating argument of my undergraduate research in a public-facing, visually driven format for an academic audience at the University of Texas at Arlington. The poster examines the power imbalance between Meta — which controls the platform's architecture — and the families left to manage safety with tools that largely do not work. Drawing on data from former Meta executive Arturo Bejar, the poster foregrounds a striking finding: 64% of Instagram's 47 parental safety tools are ineffective, and only 17% work as advertised. The poster then presents a human-centered framework for reform, proposing four actionable solutions: adoption of third-party monitoring tools, increased parental accountability, family digital-usage agreements, and community and policy-level intervention. This project demonstrates my ability to communicate complex research with clarity, purpose, and public impact.
